About Runway

Runway is built on the idea that AI is helpful as a logical perspective. It generates a prompt for an LLM to be transparent about the evidence behind recommendations. Manual research is cumbersome, so Runway is here to help.

One of Runway's goals is teaching stronger AI usage and motivating project ideas. Customize your generated prompt, share how you would improve Runway, take its recommendations even further.

Built this while on the job market myself. Feel free to connect on LinkedIn and share ideas. Let's get this 🍞

Because I want you to make it better. This is a simple website overall, and there's a lot of room for improvement. For anyone who needs a project to improve or advise on to improve your portfolio, this is for you.

So take it. The site is four files with no build step, and a fork publishes to your own GitHub Pages URL in about two minutes. The Customizing Runway section of the README walks through all of it, including what the nine color variables at the top of the stylesheet control.

Where does the company list come from?

The company list comes from US Department of Labor LCA disclosure data: the filings an employer must make public to sponsor a work visa. Runway keeps only certified filings at the entry wage (Prevailing Wage Level I) whose occupation codes match the role you picked, then groups them by employer.

The funnel line under the table shows every number that survived each step, and the provenance link next to it gives you the raw source. Nothing in the shortlist is estimated or inferred.

Why do I run the prompt myself instead of Runway doing it?
  • You can inspect how it works and see what the AI sees.
  • Your portfolio and résumé never reach a server, because there is no server.
  • You pick the model and use your existing environment.

You can use ChatGPT, DeepSeek, decide between Claude Sonnet or Fable, whichever you prefer. Search through the prompt, customize it, paste it, what you use is yours.

What does Runway decide, and what does the LLM decide?

Runway decides specific titles under what you select. For example, "Design" includes UI/UX, Graphic, Design, and Industrial Design. Sponsorship data is public and Runway makes it presentable. The same data produces the same shortlist every time, and every number traces to a filing.

Your LLM decides what to build. That half is judgment, and it is only as good as the model you run it in and the materials you give it. Read it critically. It is a well-briefed opinion, not an answer.

How does it decide what to recommend?

The prompt hands your LLM two things: aggregate patterns across every sponsor in your role - which job titles recur, which occupations dominate, whether they hire in-house - and your own materials.

The patterns decide which skills matter. Your materials decide which of those you already show. That is why each skill in the report carries both a market reason and a line pointing at something in your own work: a rating you cannot trace is one you should not trust.

Why does it only tell me one thing to work on?

There are so many possible things to work on. One of the hardest parts of job searching is figuring out what will actually help. Job hunting generates more advice than anyone can act on.

The report commits to one direction and says why. If you disagree with the reasoning, that is useful too. Hopefully it helps you find something to start on.

What is a "standout path"?

The standouts in your role tend to have one capability that most people never build: usually the one that lets you stop depending on someone else to make your work real. It is deliberately harder than the main recommendation.

It is about what to do, not who to be. Each path is evidenced by a pattern across people who visibly stand out in the field: it only ships if we can point at three or more proven practitioners who share that capability, each with a link. Those people are the receipts — there so you can trace the recommendation back and verify it if you would rather not take it on trust, not because you need to become them. The report recommends the highest-ranked path you do not already have and shows the rest so you can check the choice.

Why does the report sometimes say it could not read something?

Because AI should show where it sources its info. Every report ends with a record of whether Runway actually read your portfolio and résumé, and whether it researched the companies.

A report built without your portfolio is inference, not observation. Saying so is the difference between advice you can weigh and advice you have to take on faith.

Does a filing mean the company is hiring right now?

No. A certified LCA means an employer sponsored this occupation at this wage level at some point - not that a role is open today, and not that they will sponsor you. Treat the shortlist as "has sponsored before," which is still far better evidence than a list of companies that merely sound international.

The caveats are in the report as a reminder.

What happens to my portfolio and résumé?

Nothing leaves this page: no upload, no account, no database, no server to send anything to. The site is static files.

Your résumé is a file path, not the file: Runway never opens it. The path travels into the prompt text so that an LLM with file access can read it when you run it.

When would this be wrong?

The advice layer is a language model reading your work: it can misjudge what a case study proves, especially the difference between a polished concept and something real people used. Advice is meant to be a logical perspective based on data the model finds. Logical people can be wrong too, and right/wrong is what you make of it.

Several things weaken accuracy. Some roles have thin data: a narrow role in one quarter may rest on a few dozen filings, and small samples mislead. Runway normalises employer names conservatively, so one company can appear twice.
