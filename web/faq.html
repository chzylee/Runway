How this works

The short version: Runway finds the companies, your own LLM writes the advice, and you can see every fact that passes between them.

Where does the company list come from?

US Department of Labor LCA disclosure data - the filings an employer must make public to sponsor a work visa. Runway keeps only certified filings at the entry wage (Prevailing Wage Level I) whose occupation codes match the role you picked, then groups them by employer.

The funnel line under the table shows every number that survived each step, and the provenance link next to it gives you the raw source. Nothing in the shortlist is estimated or inferred.

Why do I run the prompt myself instead of Runway doing it?

Three reasons, in order of how much they matter. You see every fact that gets sent, because you are the one pasting it. Your portfolio and résumé never reach a server, because there is no server. And you pick the model, rather than inheriting whichever one we could afford.

The trade is real: it is one extra step. We think seeing the inputs is worth more than saving the paste, for advice you are meant to act on.

What does Runway decide, and what does the LLM decide?

Runway decides who sponsors. That half is deterministic: the same data produces the same shortlist every time, and every number traces to a filing.

Your LLM decides what to build. That half is judgment, and it is only as good as the model you run it in and the materials you give it. Read it critically. It is a well-briefed opinion, not an answer.

How does it decide what to recommend?

The prompt hands your LLM two things: aggregate patterns across every sponsor in your role - which job titles recur, which occupations dominate, whether they hire in-house - and your own materials.

The patterns decide which skills matter. Your materials decide which of those you already show. That is why each skill in the report carries both a market reason and a line pointing at something in your own work: a rating you cannot trace is one you should not trust.

Why does it only tell me one thing to work on?

Because you are not short of options. Job hunting generates more advice than anyone can act on, and a report that hands you five more directions has added to the problem it claims to solve.

The report commits to one direction and says why. If you disagree with the reasoning, that is useful too - it is written to be argued with.

What is a "standout path"?

The capability the standouts in your role tend to have and most people never build - usually the one that stops you depending on someone else to make your work real. It is deliberately harder than the main recommendation.

These are curated by hand, not generated. A path only ships if we can point at companies hiring for it or a job title that demonstrably exists. Advice that only appears in "skills you need in 2026" listicles does not qualify. The report recommends the highest-ranked one you do not already have, and shows the rest so you can check the choice.

Why does the report sometimes say it could not read something?

Because it could not, and you should know. Every report ends with a record of whether your portfolio and résumé were actually read and whether the companies were researched.

A report built without your portfolio is inference, not observation. Saying so is the difference between advice you can weigh and advice you have to take on faith.

Does a filing mean the company is hiring right now?

No. A certified LCA means an employer sponsored this occupation at this wage level at some point - not that a role is open today, and not that they will sponsor you. Treat the shortlist as "has sponsored before," which is still far better evidence than a list of companies that merely sound international.

The caveats above the table say this in full, and they travel with the report.

What happens to my portfolio and résumé?

Nothing leaves this page. There is no upload, no account, no database, and no server to send anything to - the site is static files.

Your résumé is a file path, not the file: Runway never opens it. The path travels into the prompt text so that an LLM with file access can read it when you run it.

When would this be wrong?

Several ways worth knowing. Some roles have thin data - a narrow role in one quarter may rest on a few dozen filings, and small samples mislead. Employer names are normalised conservatively, so one company can appear twice.

And the advice layer is a language model reading your work: it can misjudge what a case study proves, especially the difference between a polished concept and something real people used. That is why every claim about you is supposed to point at what it was read from. If one does not, distrust it.
